Chunk Lifecycle

Streams hex chunks — and their shared edges and corners — in and out around the focused chunk, so only a bounded neighbourhood of the infinite world is ever resident.

Problem

The world is unbounded but memory and the GPU are not. World keeps only a disc of chunks around the avatar's focused chunk: it loads chunks as the focus moves toward them and unloads them once they fall far behind. Each chunk's shared boundaries — the edges between two chunks and the corners between three — are derived entities with their own dependent lifetime: they exist only while every chunk they touch is resident.

Architecture

A chunk moves through a fixed cycle. WORLD_FOCUS_CHANGED (see world-reference-change.html) is the clock: each focus move rebuilds the load queue from the spiral of chunks within MAX_LOADED_DISTANCE of the new focus, then evicts whatever has drifted too far. Loading is sliced across idle time so a burst of new chunks never blocks a frame.

Edges and corners are not queued or distance-tracked on their own; they ride on the chunks they connect. When a chunk loads, World tries to build the boundary entities it owns, and asks its neighbours to retry theirs — each is created only once all the chunks it touches are resident, and destroyed the moment any of them unloads. To build each shared boundary exactly once, a chunk owns only half of its ring: its three upper edges (NE, N, NW) and three corners (E, NE, NW). The lower neighbour owns the shared one.

Orange edges and green corners are the ones this chunk builds; grey ones belong to a neighbour. Because exactly one of the two (edge) or three (corner) sharers claims each boundary, no shared geometry is ever built twice.

Key rules

ConcernRule
Load radiusOn every focus change, loadQueue = focus.spiral(MAX_LOADED_DISTANCE) (= 4) minus already-loaded chunks. Spiral order is closest-first, so the queue drains nearest chunks first.
Load schedulingDrained in requestIdleCallback, loading chunks only while deadline.timeRemaining() > 1ms, then re-scheduling. Loading never blocks a frame.
Unload — distanceAny chunk whose hex distance from the focus exceeds MAX_TRACKED_DISTANCE (= 10) is unloaded.
Unload — count capIf more than MAX_TRACKED_COUNT (= 50) chunks remain, the farthest are unloaded down to the cap.
HysteresisLoad distance (4) ≪ keep distance (10). A chunk loaded once lingers well past the load radius, so walking back and forth across a boundary doesn't thrash load/unload.
Edge ownershipA chunk owns edges NE, N, NW only. ChunkEdgeId.involvedChunkIds() = [owner, neighbor(edgeIdx)].
Corner ownershipA chunk owns corners E, NE, NW only. involvedChunkIds() returns the three chunks meeting at that corner.
Boundary build gateAn edge/corner is instantiated only when every involved chunk is loaded, and removed when any involved chunk unloads. On load, a chunk also retries the boundaries owned by its already-present neighbours.
CoordinatesAxial (q, r); distanceTo via cube coords. Mesh data is stored relative to referenceChunkId; positions shift on a reference change, not on load/unload.

Why two thresholds, and why loading is deferred to idle time
The split between MAX_LOADED_DISTANCE (4) and MAX_TRACKED_DISTANCE (10) is deliberate hysteresis. If a chunk were unloaded the instant it left the load radius, an avatar loitering on a chunk boundary would load and unload the same ring every few steps — re-running WASM init_chunk, rebuilding meshes, and re-deriving edges/corners each time. The wide keep-band absorbs that jitter; the MAX_TRACKED_COUNT cap is the backstop that still bounds memory if the avatar travels in a straight line and the band never naturally evicts. Loading is drained through requestIdleCallback rather than done eagerly because a single focus change can enqueue the whole spiral at once (up to the ring count for radius 4). Building them synchronously would spike frame time; slicing by deadline.timeRemaining() spreads the cost across whatever idle budget each frame has, at the price of chunks popping in over a few frames — an acceptable trade for a steady frame rate.
